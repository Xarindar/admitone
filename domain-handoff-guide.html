Before you start

First: you might not need this

A domain is the address people type to reach your site — yourbusiness.com. If you already own one, this guide gets it ready to point at your new site. If you don't, nothing here holds up the project: every Railway project comes with a free web address (something like yourbusiness.up.railway.app), and your site can launch there while you decide. A domain can be connected any time, before or after launch.

No domain yet?

Then you're already done — skip this guide and enjoy the free afternoon. If you'd like help picking and buying a domain, mention it in your kickoff email and we'll point you somewhere sensible.

One honest note before we begin: domains are sold by dozens of companies — GoDaddy, Namecheap, Squarespace, Cloudflare, Hover, IONOS, and plenty more — and every one of them arranges its menus differently. So instead of chasing screenshots of someone else's dashboard, this guide shows you what to find, what to check, and what to send us. Where the buttons live is different for everyone; what they do is the same everywhere.

Step 1

Find out where your domain lives

Every domain is managed by a company called a registrar. Find yours.

The registrar is wherever the domain was bought — it's who sends the renewal bill. Sometimes that's obvious. Sometimes the domain was set up years ago by a cousin who "knows computers." Either way, a few minutes of detective work settles it.

  1. Search your email inbox for your domain name. Look for messages with words like renewal, receipt, or expiration. The sender is usually your registrar.
  2. If nothing turns up, go to lookup.icann.org — the official, free lookup for who manages any domain on the internet.
  3. Type your domain into the search box and click Lookup.
  4. In the results, find the line labeled Registrar, near the top. That company is who you're looking for.
  5. If the registrar's name doesn't ring any bells, ask whoever set up your last website — the domain may live in their account. That's common, and fixable.
  6. Write the registrar's name down. You'll send it to us in Step 3.
If the domain is in someone else's account

No panic — this happens all the time with old web people and former developers. Mention it in your Step 3 email. Moving a domain into your own account is a well-worn path, and we'll help you walk it.

Step 2

Give the account a quick health check

Five checks that prevent every classic launch-day surprise.

Launch day goes smoothly when the account behind the domain is in order. None of these checks change anything — you're just making sure the keys work before you need them.

  1. Go to your registrar's website and sign in. If you need a password reset by email, do it now — not during launch week.
  2. Find your domain inside the account. Look for a section called My Domains, Domain List, or just Domains.
  3. Check the expiration date. If it's less than 60 days away, renew it while you're here.
  4. Make sure auto-renew is turned on. An expired domain takes the whole site down with it, and getting one back can be slow and expensive.
  5. Check that the email address on the account is one you actually read. Renewal and security notices go there.
  6. Last, look for a section named DNS, DNS Settings, DNS Management, or Advanced DNS. Don't change anything — just confirm you can see it. That's where launch day happens.
What is DNS, anyway?

DNS is the internet's address book. Small entries called records tell browsers "this domain points to that website." At launch we add two: one that points your domain at the new site, and one that proves to the host you own it. Everything else in there stays untouched — email and anything else on the domain keep working as they are.

Step 4

Choose how the changes get made

Three good routes. Pick the one that feels most comfortable.

When launch day comes, two small entries get added in your registrar's DNS settings. Any of these routes gets them there — tell us your pick in the Step 3 email, and you can change your mind later.

  • You paste the records

    We email you the exact entries — copy-paste values and where each one goes in the menus you found in Step 2. About ten minutes in your registrar's DNS settings, with us on standby while you do it.

  • You give us temporary access

    Most registrars can grant a helper limited access without ever sharing your password — GoDaddy calls it Delegate Access, Namecheap calls it Share Access, Cloudflare calls it Members. Search your registrar's help pages for "delegate access" or "share access" to find its version. We make the changes, you revoke the access after launch.

  • We do it together on a call

    A fifteen-minute screen share: you drive, we navigate. Nobody has to learn what a CNAME record is, and you watch every change as it happens.

Whichever route you pick

The change itself is small, and your site's Railway address keeps working the whole time — so there's no scary in-between moment where nothing is live.

All done

Your domain is ready for launch day

We know where the domain lives, the account behind it is healthy, and there's an agreed route for the switch. Here's how the day itself plays out: we send or make the two DNS entries, and the internet takes a little while to notice — usually minutes, occasionally up to a day or two for stragglers. Your site stays reachable at its Railway address throughout, and email on the domain isn't touched.
